# Main Results

## Overview of the Study Workflow and Datasets
![Figure 1](Figure%201.png)

This study aimed to develop and validate an artificial intelligence model, the West China Hospital fumarate hydratase-deficient renal cell carcinoma predictive model (WCH-FHPM), for the prediction of FH-dRCC from H&E-stained whole-slide images (WSIs). A total of five distinct datasets were included in this study: Dataset 1 for model development (112 slides), Dataset 2 for internal testing (365 slides), Dataset 3 for multi-center testing (114 slides), Dataset 4 for validation using data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA, 244 slides), and Dataset 5 for proof-of-concept validation (179 slides). The overarching workflow is illustrated in **Figure 1**. Briefly, WSIs were preprocessed and tessellated into 512 × 512 pixel non-overlapping patches at 40× magnification. Patches recognized as tumor or tumor-associated tissues were fed into the WCH-FHPM to compute patch-level probabilities, which were subsequently aggregated to yield slide-level prediction probabilities. Model performance was evaluated comprehensively across various clinical contexts, followed by comparisons with human pathologists and exploration of AI-assisted diagnostic strategies. Additionally, the morphological basis of the model's predictive decisions was analyzed, and an online web platform was built and validated in a real-world multi-institutional setting.

## Performance of the WCH-FHPM in Multiple Validation Datasets
![Figure 2](Figure%202.png)

The diagnostic performance of WCH-FHPM for predicting FH-dRCC was systematically validated in multiple independent datasets (**Figure 2**). ROC curve analyses demonstrated robust discriminative performance in the internal testing set (Dataset 2, N=365; **Figure 2A**), the multi-center testing set (Dataset 3, N=114; **Figure 2B**), and the TCGA external validation cohort (Dataset 4, N=244; **Figure 2C**). Using a predefined probability threshold of 0.5, the confusion matrices (**Figure 2D-F**) further detailed the precise classification results on these respective datasets, confirming the model's consistent capability in identifying true positive and true negative cases across varied data sources.

## Human-Machine Collaborative Strategies Enhance Pathologists' Decision-Making
![Figure 3](Figure%203.png)

To investigate the utility of WCH-FHPM in a clinical setting, we compared its predictive ability with that of experienced pathologists and evaluated three distinct human-machine collaborative strategies (**Figure 3**). These strategies included: (A) the **AND strategy**, where a slide is deemed FH-dRCC positive only if both the pathologist and the model agree on the diagnosis; (B) the **OR strategy**, classifying a slide as positive if either the pathologist or the model supports an FH-dRCC diagnosis; and (C) the **Modified strategy**, where pathologists formulate an initial independent diagnosis and are then allowed to adjust their assessments after referring to the model's predictions. The comparative ROC analysis illustrated significant enhancements in the pathologists' overall diagnostic capability and accuracy when assisted by the WCH-FHPM, corroborating the clinical added value of integrating our AI tool into the pathological workflow.

## Interpretability of WCH-FHPM Based on Pathological Morphology
![Figure 4](Figure%204.png)

To elucidate the "black box" nature of the WCH-FHPM, we investigated the underlying morphological features driving its predictions (**Figure 4**). Model interpretability was visualized through prediction score heatmaps alongside corresponding microscopic morphological reviews (**Figure 4A**). High scores (red) indicated regions strongly predictive of FH-dRCC, whereas low scores (blue) indicated non-FH-dRCC. 
Further quantitative analysis was conducted on 1,600 patches sampled from 80 WSIs, stratified by high and low prediction scores. We identified 11 distinct histopathological features—categorized into tumor characteristics and stromal characteristics—that showed statistically significant differences in incidence rates between model-predicted FH-dRCC and non-FH-dRCC (including papillary RCC and non-pRCC) profiles (*P* < 0.05 to ***P* < 0.001, **Figure 4B**). We expanded this slide-level morphological feature analysis in an independent cohort of 310 slides from Datasets 2 and 3 (93 FH-dRCC and 217 non-FH-dRCC; **Figure 4C**). Finally, the predictive superiority of the deep learning model WCH-FHPM was reinforced when compared to two traditional morphology-based criteria models: Model 1 (relying on classic features such as eosinophilic macronucleoli and foamy macrophages) and Model 2 (incorporating additional stromal features like stromal reaction, hyaline degeneration, and myxoid change) (**Figure 4D**).

## Development and Proof-of-Concept Validation of an Online AI Platform
![Figure 5](Figure%205.png)

To facilitate future clinical adoption and global multicenter collaboration, we constructed a user-friendly and intuitive web platform powered by the proposed WCH-FHPM (https://wchrcc.ai4ss.com/) (**Figure 5A**). This platform allows clinicians and pathologists to upload localized H&E WSIs directly and instantly acquire the AI's predicted probability of FH-dRCC.  
For clinical translation viability, a proof-of-concept validation was conducted using Dataset 5 (**Figure 5B**), in collaboration with the Rare Kidney Cancer Collaborative Group, the Genitourinary Cancer Committee, and the China Anti-Cancer Association. The platform consistently achieved high predictive accuracy on this real-world dataset, as demonstrated by the ROC metrics, sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value (PPV), and negative predictive value (NPV). These results highlight the practicality, reliability, and promising prospects for the clinical deployment of WCH-FHPM as an assistive tool for pathologists worldwide.
